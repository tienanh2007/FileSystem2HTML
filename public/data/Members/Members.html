Karthik Vishwanath

I completed my bachelor's degree in Physics and Mathematics in 1997 from St. Josephs College, Bangalore, India. Since I was quite attracted to computing and learning about computing, I landed a job with WIPRO Systems Inc. After a brief sojourn of 6 months on the corporate "bench", life inside a software company was not as I'd imagined. I joined Dr. Upi Bhalla's group as a junior research fellow at the National Center for Biological Sciences in 1998. I decided to move to the United States to seek a graduate degree in Physics looking for research project with computational emphasis and addressing biological problems.

I received a MS in Physics from Dartmouth College in 2003 and completed my Ph.D from the University of Michigan with Dr. Mary-Ann Mycek in 2005. I held post-doctoral research positions at Michigan till 2006 and then at Duke till 2012. I joined the Optics and Photonics staff at Radiation Monitoring Devices, Inc. as a senior research scientist in 2012 till I found my position at Miami University in 2014.

I direct the OSIM lab here where we explore the use of optics to understand and solve biomedical problems. I like working with students and researchers across disciplinary boundaries toward applying science and technology for improving quality of human life.

Anthony Young

Undergraduate degree: Biological Physics, Miami University
Research Project: Multi-wavelength speckle imaging
At OSIM from: 2014
My name is Anthony Young, and I am a fourth-year student at Miami. I am completing a combined Bachelor’s/Master’s degree program in the Physics Department. I have been working in the OSIM lab with Dr. Vishwanath since the fall of 2014. Performing research has allowed me to explore scientific phenomena that I would not have been able to in a classroom setting. In the lab, I get to work alongside others who possess similar interests and offer strong encouragement. I am studying the applicability of laser speckle contrast imaging (LSCI), a non-invasive, optical technique, to measure superficial blood flow in biological tissue. I have had the opportunity to present my research on LSCI at Miami’s Undergraduate Research Forum, the 2015 OSAPS annual meeting in Cleveland, and the 2016 SPIE Photonics West conference in San Francisco. My current project is to investigate a technique capable of completing LSCI flow measurements using multiple wavelengths of light simultaneously.

Sara Zanfardino

Undergraduate degree: Biochemistry (major); Physics (minor), Miami University
Research Project: Diffuse correlation spectroscopy
At OSIM from: 2016
My name is Sara Zanfardino and I am a first year graduate student here at Miami University. I also completed my Bachelor’s degree at Miami University where I majored in Biochemistry and minored in Physics. My biochemistry background combined with my desire to deepen my knowledge in physics is what led me to the Optical Spectroscopy and Imaging in Medicine (OSIM) lab here, concentrating on biomedical applications of physics using optical tools. My research in the OSIM lab is focused on Diffuse Correlation Spectroscopy (DCS). I investigate how blood flow parameters can be determined by directing laser light onto a sample and then detecting the light after it has ‘diffused’ through the sample and monitoring induced or naturally occurring changes. In the future I plan to continue my education in physics and hopefully use it to increase student interest in the vastly emerging field.

Adam Eaton

Class of: 2018
Major(s): Physics
At OSIM from: 2015
In OSIM Lab, I work with Dr. Vishwanath and Vinoin Vincely on testing whether it is possible to obtain a person’s heart-rate using only a video of them. One major challenge we have been facing on this project is subject movement. As the person being filmed moves, our methods generally produce poorer results than if they were motionless. The main goal of this project is to be able to extract accurate heart-rates from all subjects – even moving ones. Doing this would have numerous potential applications ranging from remote healthcare to psychological experiments. I joined OSIM in May 2016, and my time here has been positive in every way. Since most of the lab groups are small, there is always an opportunity to get some valuable, hands-on experience learning how “real-world” science is done. One of my favorite things about OSIM is that it is open to all students – not just graduate students and upperclassmen. The work I hope to continue working with OSIM for many semesters to come.
